# Back and Knee Safe Toning Plan

> [!warning] Medical clearance required
> This is a conservative planning framework for review by your surgeon, physician, or physical therapist. With multiple herniated discs, chronic sciatica, spinal damage, prior knee surgeries, and planned back surgery, do not begin or progress exercises that have not been cleared for your current phase of care.

## Goal

Maintain muscle activity and gently improve tone without impact, heavy loading, deep knee bending, spinal twisting, or pushing through neurological symptoms.

## Readiness Check Before Every Session

Do not start if any item below is new or worse than normal:

- Radiating back or neck pain
- Numbness or tingling
- Arm or leg weakness
- Knee swelling, buckling, or locking
- Dizziness, chest pain, or shortness of breath at rest

Seek urgent medical care for new bowel or bladder dysfunction, inability to urinate, saddle-area numbness, or significant/progressive weakness.

## Intensity Rules

- Effort: easy to light, approximately 2–4 out of 10
- Pain during exercise: no sharp pain and no increase in radiating symptoms
- Movement: slow, supported, and controlled
- Start with one set; increase only after several symptom-stable sessions and medical approval
- Rest 45–90 seconds between movements
- Stop if symptoms rise during the session or remain worse afterward

## Weekly Framework

| Day | Session |
|---|---|
| Monday | Supported upper body + gentle core activation |
| Tuesday | Approved low-impact movement or recovery |
| Wednesday | Supported lower body + knee stabilizers |
| Thursday | Recovery and mobility prescribed by clinician |
| Friday | Full-body light circuit |
| Saturday | Approved low-impact movement or rest |
| Sunday | Rest and weekly symptom review |

## Session A — Supported Upper Body

Complete only movements specifically cleared for you.

1. Seated shoulder-blade squeeze — 5 to 8 controlled repetitions
2. Seated resistance-band row with neutral spine — 5 to 8 repetitions
3. Seated light biceps curl, back supported — 5 to 8 repetitions
4. Seated band triceps press-down — 5 to 8 repetitions
5. Gentle hand-grip work — 8 to 10 repetitions
6. Abdominal bracing in a clinician-approved position — 3 to 5 holds of 5 seconds

Avoid overhead pressing, unsupported bent-over rows, shrugging under load, forceful neck movement, and breath-holding.

## Session B — Supported Lower Body

1. Seated quadriceps tightening or quad set — 5 to 8 holds of 5 seconds
2. Seated knee extension through a comfortable, clinician-approved range — 5 to 8 repetitions
3. Seated heel raises — 8 to 10 repetitions
4. Seated toe raises — 8 to 10 repetitions
5. Supported standing hip abduction with very small range — 5 to 8 repetitions per side
6. Supported sit-to-stand from a high, firm chair — only if cleared; 3 to 5 repetitions

Avoid lunges, deep squats, kneeling, jumping, running, step-downs, unstable surfaces, and loaded knee twisting unless your clinician authorizes them.

## Session C — Light Full-Body Circuit

Perform one round initially:

1. Seated shoulder-blade squeeze — 5 repetitions
2. Seated band row — 5 repetitions
3. Seated biceps curl — 5 repetitions
4. Quad set — 5 holds
5. Seated heel raise — 8 repetitions
6. Approved abdominal brace — 3 holds

The session should finish with the same or lower neurological symptoms than it started with.

## Aerobic Options to Ask the Clinician About

- Short, level walking intervals with support
- Recumbent bicycle
- Pool walking or aquatic therapy
- Seated pedal exerciser
- Arm ergometer

Begin with 3–5 minutes if cleared. Add time gradually rather than increasing resistance or speed.

## Progression Gate

Progress only when all conditions are met:

- Cleared by the treating clinician
- No increase in sciatica, numbness, weakness, knee swelling, or instability
- Pain returns to baseline by later that day and is not worse the next morning
- Technique remains controlled
- At least three symptom-stable sessions completed

Progress one variable at a time: first repetitions, then an additional set, then very light resistance.

## Stop and Contact the Clinician

- New or increased radiating pain
- New numbness or tingling
- New weakness or foot drop
- Knee buckling, locking, or significant swelling
- Pain that remains elevated the next day
- Loss of balance or function
